BA4 Microwave Multiplexing Readout and RF Cable Routing Design

2022 Jan 26 — Tom Liu

Introduction

The fourth receiver of the BICEP Array (BA4) plans to have approximately 20000 detectors in its receiver. This significant increase in detector count imposes unique challenges to the readout of the detector signnal, leading to designs different from the previous BICEP Array readout systems.

Because BA4 consists of more detectors than the other BA receivers, there is a much higher required multiplexing factor to mechanically fit the readout system into the cryostat. A multiplexing factor of 1000 would require about 24 readout chains, which leads to a total of 48 channels, with half of the lines being input probe tones provided by SMuRF electronics, and the other half being output modulations on those probe tones.

Here, we discuss the thermal, electrical, and mechanical constraints of our design problem and present the first iteration of the RF cable routing design, primarily consisting of semi-rigid coaxial cables.

Overall Design

The overall RF chain schematic is similar to the BKumux readout chain. We have stainless steel in the warm stages as they are a relatively cheap material while retaining an appropriate level of signal integrity and minimal thermal conductance. The colder stages require Cupronickel for the input lines to further decrease the thermal load, while the output lines require superconducting Niobium Titanium to preserve the integrity of the signal.

To decrease the input probe tone to the necessary power level while preventing the signals from succumbing to thermal noise, several attenuators are spread throughout the RF chain. DC blocks are scattered throughout the chain to both remove unwanted voltage bias while also providing thermal breaks for the inner conductor of the coaxial cables. Amplifiers are placed on the 4K and 50K stage so that the output signal is discernable once it reaches the room temperature electronics. Various other components like directional couplers, bias tees, and circulators are currently included in the design, but pending tests to determine their impact on the overall readout system, these components may be removed.

Electrical Considerations

The electrical considerations include ensuring that the input probe tones are at an appropriate power level once they reach the focal plane for optimal resonance with the SQUID resonators, optimizing the signal-to-noise ratio, and minimizing reflections and impedance mismatches.

The input power of the probe tones are at approximately -32 dBm per tone, which corresponds to about 0.63 \( \mu W \). The resonators at the focal plane operate optimally with -75 dBm tones, so we use attenuators to decrease the power at every stage of the readout chain. Spreading out the attenuators over multiple stages provides a larger overall thermal load on the cryostage stages, but having a large attenuation at the warmer stages risks the warmer thermal noise becoming a significant franction of the overall power.

Various other components of the RF chain also produce attenuation. The RF cables have some attenuation depending on their materials and are proportional to the length of the cables. Other components like the DC blocks and bulkheads cause some reflections due to impedance mismatches, and they are upperbounded by the maximum VSWR of the components.

The directional coupler acts as an attenuator with additional heatsinking; the electrical signal is inductively carried forward, so there is no physical connection between the inner connductors of the coaxial cables of the warmer and colder side of the directional coupler. The Bias Tee acts as a DC block, and like the directional coupler, also provides additional heatsinking. Tests are undergoing to determine whether a heatsunk attenuator or DC block can serve the same purpose.

The thermal noise can be modeled by the Johnson-Nyquist noise given by the following: \[ N=k_B T\Delta f \] where \(N\) is the noise power, \(k_B\) is the Boltzman constant, \(T\) is temperature, and \(\Delta f\) is the bandwidth. We can convert the noise power into units \(pA \) per \( \sqrt{Hz} \) with the given 50 ohm impedance. We compare this to the power of the RF tones to determine a signal-to-noise ratio. The superconducting cables should not have any resistance, but the impedance of other non-superconducting components might cause Johnson noise.

Thermal Considerations

To properly operate, the detector modules need to be at approximately 0.25K, which ultimately determines the thermal budget for each stage of the cryostat. The two main forms of thermal loading in our RF chain are conductive and radiative. Conduction occurs when a warmer temperature physically transfers energy to a cooler stage, and for our RF chain, the cables provide physical connections. The conduction can be approximated with the following equation: \[ P = k \frac{\Delta T}{L}\] where \(P\) is the power transfered, \(k\) is the thermal conductivity, \(\Delta T\) is the difference in temperature between the connected stages, and \(L\) is the length of the cables. Note that the \(k\) is the thermal conductivity in units given by the Coax-Co cable catalogues. The thermal conductivity depends on temperature, increasing with increasing temperature. We use the more pessimistic approximation by assuming the warmer thermal conductivity for each connection between the temperature stages.

Dissipative loading occurs when the resistance of the cables dissipate the power of the signal as it travels. Again, we produce the pessimistic estimates by assuming all the power is dissipated onto the colder stage. We also assume that all reflections caused by impedance mismatches are eventually dissipated, another overestimate of the thermal loading. The amount of dissipation is calculated by subtracting the input power from the output power at each component.

Mechanical Considerations

One of the primary challenges of using semi-rigid coaxial cables is fitting all the necessary wiring into the limited space provided by the cryostat. To accomodate the housekeeping electronics, we chose to distribute the cables over three sections, where each section was half incoming and half outgoing signals. Below, we outline the general placement of the wires and describe the assembly process.

Warm Stage Assembly Procedure

The warm stages are connected by two stages of cabling: from 300K to 50K, and from 50K to 4K. The cabling is intending to be nearly entirely assembled outside of the cryostat by attaching the cables to the feedthroughs, then inserting the cable-feedthrough subassembly into the warm stages.

We first build the 4K feedthrough section on the bench. Set up two styrofoam pieces on the side, then insert the female-female gold plated SMA bulkheads into the appropriate holes. Then, insert the DC connectors onto the feedthrough ports.

Next, we build the 50K feedthrough section. Similarly, set up pieces of styrofoam to hold up the feedthrough plate, then insert the female-female bulkheads into the holes closer to the DC ports. Assemble the amplifier chain separately by attaching the 4K_to_50K cable, amplifier bracket, and male-female bulkhead onto the amplifier. Ensure that the output of the amplifier is directed toward the SMA bulkhead. Insert the amplifier chain into the holes further from the DC feedthrough, then torque the bulkheads. Attach the 10 dB attenuators and the 50K_to_4K cables onto the remaining bulkheads.

We now connect the 50K feedthrough to the 4K feedthrough. Screw the assembly rod onto the 50K feedthrough plate, then screw the 4K feedthrough on top of the assembly rod. Insert the two aligning bolts to align the feedthroughs, then connect the cables onto the 4K plate. Clamp the DC wiring onto the DC ports. Finally, screw the entire 50K to 4K section into the appropriate slot in the warm stages of the cryostat. Remove the assembly rods and aligners. Repeat this process for all three sections.

We now assemble the 300K feedthrough. Insert the bulkheads onto the 300K feedthrough plate, then attach the 300K_to_50K cables. Screw the feedthrough onto the bucket, then tighten the dangling RF cables onto the 50K feedthrough bulkheads. Connect the DC wiring to the ports. Repeat this process for all three sections. Note that the center feedthrough section cables are different from the side section cables.

While this assembly is technically possible, it may be time consuming to torque 48 SMA connections for each strage of the cryostat. We are looking into alternatives to SMA connections, such as Ardent Solution's multicoaxial cables and Delft's multichannel strips. Price permitting, these new technologies could greatly simplify the assembly process.

Cold Stage Components

The cold subK stages would be assmbled outside of the cryostat and placed on top of the 4K baseplate, so the assembly is relatively more straightforward. The most difficult component to attach would be the circulators that are bolted to the IC stage. The insertion of the amplifiers onto the 4K upper plate also requires special attention. Cryostat Modifications

Below, we provide summary of all the parts that will be modified. We attempt to minimize the modifications to the existing cryostat.

300K Stage

The only required change in the 300K stage is the feedthroughs. We drill 16 holes in each feedthrough for the SMA bulkheads and 4 wide slots to fit the DC connectors.

50K Stage

Similarly to the 300K stage, the modifications are limitted to the feedthroughs. We drill the holes for the SMA bulkheads and the amplfier brackets. We cut out 4 DC feedthroughs holes instead of 5. Finally, there is an extra lip for the assembly rod to fit in.

4K Base Stage

We cut out a section of the 4K baseplate to accomodate the feedthroughs, then machine a new feedthrough for the 4K base stage.

4K Upper Stage

We remove the previous cutouts for the MCE amplifiers and drill new holes to accomodate the SMA bulkheads. Holes are also drilled to attach the 4K amplifiers

2K Stage

The modifications are minimal. We drill 48 holes on the outer edges for the SMA bulkheads.

IC Stage

We drill the 48 SMA bulkhead holes on the outer edges and tapped holes for mounting the circulators.

UC Stage

We drill SMA bulkhead holes on the outer corners of the UC plate. Special care was taken to ensure that the RF cables do not interfere with the printed circuit board.

DC Wiring

DC wiring is necessary for providing power to the amplifiers and the detector modules. New and existing holes in the cryostat stages will accomodate the DC connectors and clamps. We plan to have connectors and filters at the 300K, 4K, and UC stages, with clamps to provide thermal contact on the other stages.
