Introduction

The BICEP Array 1 receiver (BA1) has an absorptive forebaffle attached to reduce signal contamination from the ground. The forebaffle absorbs stray light to ensure the receiver sees only signal from the sky. Unfortunately, the forebaffle itself contributes its own thermal loading onto the BA1 detectors. In this posting, we measure the thermal loading caused by the BA1 forebaffle in various configurations. In 2022, the cardiff low-bandpass filter was changed to block out the blue-leak dark coupling. A 4 icm filter was added to the existing 1.6 icm. We compare our results to previous years' forebaffle loading to determine the effectiveness of this addition.

optical efficiency Instead of plotting the power in pW, we can also plot the Rayleigh Jean Temperature, represented by \(K_{rj}\) in the pager. The RJ temperature is given by \(T_{RJ} = \frac{P}{dP/dT}\), where \(P\) is the original optical loading, and \(dP/dT\) is a parameter of each detector, representing how much change in loading is caused by a 1K increase in temperature of a hypothetical blackbody. Thus, the RJ temperature represents the equivalent temperature of the forebaffle if it were a blackbody covering the entire aperature. The tile plot is mostly orange to deep red, suggesting an RJ temperature between 3-7 K. We see that some tiles have higher RJ temperatures than others despite having similar loading. We can attribute this to a low \(dP/dT\) for those tiles, as we can see when we check the "Det params?" tile plot.

Some of the detector tiles have vastly different values from the rest. To determine the cause, we can view individual detectors below. Each Rx, Tile, and row/col is a small square in the tile plot. Each tile is either divided into 2 or 4 triangles. Two triangles means there is one detector for each polarization, 'A' and 'B'. Four means for each polarization, there are in addition 2 different bands. The different bands are only relevant for BA1. In the two side by side plots, the left plot is all the datapoints on one graph, while the right plot is the same data but plotted in a specific range. The x and y range are consistent within each receiver to provide a better idea of the data's scale.

By examining the different load curves, it becomes straightforward to cut the data based on the load curve quality. We fit two lines, one horizontal and one vertical onto the loadcurve. If all the points are within approximately 10 percent of that fitted line, we consider the curve to be good. If any load curve does not satisfy our requirements, we cut the detector.

We plot the extra loading for different baselines, showing the median and quartiles for each forebaffle configuration. We can see that having no cuts introduces many more outliers.

We also see that for the BA1 receiver, the baseline line is pretty consistent, that when there is no forebaffle on BA1, the extra loading is not visible to 0.05 pW. However, for each Keck receiver, there is a clear power drift as we process the different tags. The leading hypothesis is that because the Keck receivers are higher frequency and more susceptible to weather, they pick up slight differences in sky loading. We subtract away this offset in our final analysis.

Conclusion

We provide the median forebaffle loading for each receiver with cuts. Without cuts, the mean averages into the extreme outliers (which can be observed in the tile plots), and significantly changes the average. Without cuts, the spread is nearly larger than the value itself.

With the cuts, the values for the Keck receivers are roughly equal to the previous 2020 posting examining forebaffle loading at about 0.3 pW. However, Rx3 has a slightly larger loading at 0.5 pW, but this is consistent with Kenny's 2018 posting.

The BA1 receiver has about half as much loading as the 2020 posting, suggesting that adding the low-pass band filter has improved performance. Even though the loading itself was cut by about half, the RJ temperature has only decreased by about 1-2K. This is likely due to a slightly lower \(dP/dT\) for some of the detectors compared to 2020.
