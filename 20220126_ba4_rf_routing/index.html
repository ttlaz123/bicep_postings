BA4 Microwave Multiplexing Readout and RF Cable Routing Design

2022 Jan 26 — Tom Liu

Introduction

The fourth receiver of the BICEP Array (BA4) plans to have approximately 20000 detectors in its receiver. This significant increase in detector count imposes unique challenges to the readout of the detector signnal, leading to designs different from the previous BICEP Array readout systems.

Because BA4 consists of more detectors than the other BA receivers, there is a much higher required multiplexing factor to mechanically fit the readout system into the cryostat. A multiplexing factor of 1000 would require about 24 readout chains, which leads to a total of 48 channels, with half of the lines being input probe tones provided by SMuRF electronics, and the other half being output modulations on those probe tones.

Here, we discuss the thermal, electrical, and mechanical constraints of our design problem and present the first iteration of the RF cable routing design, primarily consisting of semi-rigid coaxial cables.

Overall Design

The overall RF chain schematic is similar to the BKumux readout chain. We have stainless steel in the warm stages as they are a relatively cheap material while retaining an appropriate level of signal integrity and minimal thermal conductance. The colder stages require Cupronickel for the input lines to further decrease the thermal load, while the output lines require superconducting Niobium Titanium to preserve the integrity of the signal.

To decrease the input probe tone to the necessary power level while preventing the signals from succumbing to thermal noise, several attenuators are spread throughout the RF chain. DC blocks are scattered throughout the chain to both remove unwanted voltage bias while also providing thermal breaks for the inner conductor of the coaxial cables. Amplifiers are placed on the 4K and 50K stage so that the output signal is discernable once it reaches the room temperature electronics. Various other components like directional couplers, bias tees, and circulators are currently included in the design, but pending tests to determine their impact on the overall readout system, these components may be removed.

Electrical Considerations

The electrical considerations include ensuring that the input probe tones are at an appropriate power level once they reach the focal plane for optimal resonance with the SQUID resonators, optimizing the signal-to-noise ratio, and minimizing reflections and impedance mismatches.

The input power of the probe tones are at approximately -32 dBm per tone, which corresponds to about 0.63 \(\mu W\). The resonators at the focal plane operate optimally with -75 dBm tones, so we use attenuators to decrease the power at every stage of the readout chain. Spreading out the attenuators over multiple stages provides a larger overall thermal load on the cryostage stages, but having a large attenuation at the warmer stages risks the warmer thermal noise becoming a significant franction of the overall power.

Various other components of the RF chain also produce attenuation. The RF cables have some attenuation depending on their materials and are proportional to the length of the cables. Other components like the DC blocks and bulkheads cause some reflections due to impedance mismatches, and they are upperbounded by the maximum VSWR of the components.

The directional coupler acts as an attenuator with additional heatsinking; the electrical signal is inductively carried forward, so there is no physical connection between the inner connductors of the coaxial cables of the warmer and colder side of the directional coupler. The Bias Tee acts as a DC block, and like the directional coupler, also provides additional heatsinking. Tests are undergoing to determine whether a heatsunk attenuator or DC block can serve the same purpose.
